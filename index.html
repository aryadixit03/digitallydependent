Digitally Dependent?

Understanding Canada's Digital Sovereignty

How do structural digital dependencies across cloud infrastructure, data, social platforms, and digital trade shape Canada's political-economic sovereignty within the U.S.-Canada relationship?

Canada's digital position is shaped by both integration and autonomy. Global cloud networks, platform ecosystems, and cross-border legal frameworks create deeply interconnected systems, while Canadian policy, talent, and emerging technologies offer opportunities to shape its digital future.

Foundations

What Is Digital Sovereignty?

The concept sits at the intersection of infrastructure, law, economics, and political strategy. It is less about complete control than about the ability to make meaningful choices inside interdependent systems.

Canada's Digital Landscape

Dependency and capacity exist at the same time

Canada's digital landscape is shaped by a mix of reliance on globally dominant firms and meaningful domestic capability in strategic technology fields.

A large share of cloud infrastructure used in Canada is operated by U.S.-based firms. Those firms often function as hyperscalers Large cloud providers operating massive globally distributed data infrastructure. that supply storage, compute, and software services at scale. One estimate suggests that roughly 60% of Canada's cloud market is controlled by U.S. hyperscalers, which shows how deeply foreign-owned infrastructure is embedded in everyday public and private digital operations.

Cross-border governance matters because infrastructure ownership can shape who has access, which contracts apply, and under which laws data may be governed. This is why debates around the CLOUD Act A U.S. law that can affect law-enforcement access requests for data held by U.S.-linked service providers. and data localization Policies that require certain data to be stored or processed within a given jurisdiction. continue to matter in public-sector procurement and data policy. Some sources note that more than 500,000 Canadians now rely on Starlink for internet connectivity, illustrating how dependence is not limited to cloud services but extends into communications infrastructure as well.

Platform ecosystems across search, social media, app stores, and digital advertising are also shaped by large global firms. That affects Canadian policy space, market structure, and the visibility of domestic content and journalism. In practice, sovereignty questions arise not only from where systems are located, but from who sets the terms of access, who captures the revenue, and which institutions have leverage when markets become highly concentrated.

Canada also has meaningful capacity in areas such as AI, satellite systems, photonics, and quantum technologies. These strengths do not eliminate dependence, but they do create options for industrial strategy, research leadership, and public capacity. Some people argue that the core question is whether Canada will build enough sovereign infrastructure to keep high-value firms, data, and communications systems anchored under Canadian control.

The key policy question is not whether Canada can build every layer of the stack alone. It is whether Canada can identify strategically important layers where domestic capability meaningfully improves resilience, bargaining power, and governance flexibility. In that sense, domestic capability matters most when it strengthens legal jurisdiction, procurement leverage, and long-term control over strategic technologies.
